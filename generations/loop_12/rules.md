# Loop 12 Cellular Automata Rules: Classical General Relativity

This document contains 10 distinct, mathematically rigorous cellular automata (CA) rules focused on the domain of **Classical General Relativity**. 

Unlike the stochastic processes in Loop 11 or the cryptographic scramblers in Loop 10, the rules in this loop model the core physical and geometric concepts of Einstein's theory of gravitation. These rules operate on a 1D lattice where the state of cell $x$ at discrete time step $t$ is denoted by $S_t(x) \in \{0, 1, \dots, N-1\}$. The rules simulate phenomena such as matter-geometry coupling, gravitational wave propagation and scattering, event horizons, frame dragging, gravitational redshift, and cosmological expansion.

## Rule Definitions

### Rule 1: Wheeler's Loop (Matter-Geometry Coupling)
* **States ($N$):** $8$ states ($0, 1, \dots, 7$).
  * The state $S_t(x)$ is split arithmetically into:
    * Matter presence: $M_t(x) = S_t(x) \pmod{2} \in \{0, 1\}$
    * Gravitational potential (geometry): $G_t(x) = \lfloor S_t(x) / 2 \rfloor \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  Let the local gravitational potential be $\Phi(y) = G_t(y)$. Matter at cell $y \in N(x)$ moves toward neighbors with higher potential. We define direction flags for each cell $y$:
  * $L(y) = \mathbb{I}(\Phi(y-1) > \Phi(y) \land \Phi(y-1) \ge \Phi(y+1))$
  * $R(y) = \mathbb{I}(\Phi(y+1) > \Phi(y) \land \Phi(y+1) > \Phi(y-1))$
  * $S(y) = 1 - L(y) - R(y)$ (Stay flag)
  
  The updated matter state at cell $x$ is:
  $$M_{t+1}(x) = \min\left(1, M_t(x-1) R(x-1) + M_t(x+1) L(x+1) + M_t(x) S(x)\right)$$
  
  The updated gravitational curvature is generated by the new local matter density and diffuses spatially:
  $$G_{t+1}(x) = \min\left(3, \left\lfloor \frac{G_t(x-1) + G_t(x) + G_t(x+1)}{3} \right\rfloor + M_{t+1}(x)\right)$$
  
  Recombine the components to obtain the next state:
  $$S_{t+1}(x) = M_{t+1}(x) + 2 \cdot G_{t+1}(x)$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** This models John Wheeler's classic feedback loop: *"Spacetime tells matter how to move; matter tells spacetime how to curve."* Matter moves along the local gravitational potential gradient. The potential is sourced by matter density and undergoes local diffusion, representing the retardation and propagation of gravitational fields.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Matter seeds clump together over time, forming highly curved, stable, or slowly moving gravitating bodies. Visually, matter lines are surrounded by a symmetric "halo" of decaying potential colors.

### Rule 2: Gravitational Wave Interferometer
* **States ($N$):** $10$ states ($0, 1, \dots, 9$).
  * States $0 \dots 8$ represent wave states:
    * $u_t(x) = S_t(x) \pmod{3} \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ (Current amplitude)
    * $u_{t-1}(x) = \lfloor S_t(x) / 3 \rfloor \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ (Previous amplitude)
  * State $9$ represents a static massive lens/deflector.
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  * If $S_t(x) = 9$:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = 9$$
  * Else:
    Let $W(y)$ be the wave amplitude filter:
    $$W(y) = \begin{cases} S_t(y) \pmod{3} & \text{if } S_t(y) < 9 \\ 0 & \text{if } S_t(y) = 9 \end{cases}$$
    
    The updated wave amplitude is solved via the discretized wave equation:
    $$u_{t+1}(x) = \left[ W(x-1) + W(x+1) - u_{t-1}(x) + 3 \right] \pmod{3}$$
    
    Recombine to obtain the next state:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = u_{t+1}(x) + 3 \cdot u_t(x)$$
    where $u_t(x) = S_t(x) \pmod{3}$.
* **Mathematical Rationale:** In the weak-field limit of GR, spacetime perturbations satisfy a second-order wave equation. By encoding the current amplitude $u_t$ and the history $u_{t-1}$ into the state, the CA computes the discrete wave equation $u_{t+1} - 2u_t + u_{t-1} = c^2 (u_t(x+1) - 2u_t + u_t(x-1))$ with speed $c=1$. Massive objects (state 9) act as absorbing/reflecting boundary walls.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Symmetrical, intersecting wave packets that propagate diagonally. When they encounter static mass cells (state 9), they form reflective wavefronts and interference fringes.

### Rule 3: Event Horizon and Singularity (Causal Collapse)
* **States ($N$):** $6$ states ($0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5$).
  * State $0$: Empty space.
  * States $1, 2, 3, 4$: Matter density.
  * State $5$: Singularity/Horizon ($H$).
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  * If $S_t(x) = 5$:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = 5$$
  * If $S_t(x-1) = 5$ and $S_t(x+1) = 5$:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = 5$$
  * If $S_t(x-1) = 5$ and $S_t(x+1) \neq 5$:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = 0$$
  * If $S_t(x+1) = 5$ and $S_t(x-1) \neq 5$:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = 0$$
  * If no singularity is adjacent:
    Let $Total = S_t(x-1) + S_t(x) + S_t(x+1)$.
    * If $Total \ge 8$:
      $$S_{t+1}(x) = 5$$
    * Else:
      * If $S_t(x) \ge S_t(x-1)$ and $S_t(x) \ge S_t(x+1)$ (and not all three are equal):
        $$S_{t+1}(x) = \min(4, S_t(x) + \mathbb{I}(S_t(x-1) > 0) + \mathbb{I}(S_t(x+1) > 0))$$
      * If $S_t(x) < S_t(x-1)$ and $S_t(x) < S_t(x+1)$:
        $$S_{t+1}(x) = \max(0, S_t(x) - 1)$$
      * Otherwise:
        $$S_{t+1}(x) = S_t(x)$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** This models runaway gravitational collapse and event horizon formation. When matter density exceeds a critical threshold ($Total \ge 8$), it collapses to form a singularity ($5$). The singularity acts as a one-way causal sink: cells adjacent to it lose their matter to the singularity ($S_{t+1} = 0$), representing geodesic flow across the event horizon.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Pockets of matter gather together, forming growing, dark lanes (black holes, state 5) which are bounded by vacuum (state 0) due to accretion.

### Rule 4: Gravitational Redshift (Time Dilation)
* **States ($N$):** $8$ states ($0, 1, \dots, 7$).
  * The state $S_t(x)$ is split into:
    * Oscillating signal state: $P_t(x) = S_t(x) \pmod{2} \in \{0, 1\}$
    * Static gravitational potential: $\Phi(x) = \lfloor S_t(x) / 2 \rfloor \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  The update frequency of the signal is gated by the potential $\Phi(x)$. Let the update probability be:
  $$P_{update}(x) = 1.0 - 0.25 \cdot \Phi(x)$$
  
  At each step, roll a random number $r_x \in [0, 1)$:
  * If $r_x < P_{update}(x)$:
    $$P_{t+1}(x) = P_t(x-1) \quad (\text{propagation})$$
  * Else:
    $$P_{t+1}(x) = P_t(x) \quad (\text{delay})$$
    
  The gravitational potential remains static:
  $$\Phi_{t+1}(x) = \Phi(x)$$
  
  Recombine components:
  $$S_{t+1}(x) = P_{t+1}(x) + 2 \cdot \Phi_{t+1}(x)$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** Time runs slower in stronger gravitational fields. This is modeled by scaling the probability of a cell updating its signal state by its local potential $\Phi$. This direct scaling mimics the Schwarzschild metric time dilation factor $d\tau = \sqrt{1 - \frac{2GM}{r}} dt$, slowing local clocks down.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Signals traveling left-to-right move quickly through flat regions ($\Phi=0$), but slow down as they pass through potential wells ($\Phi > 0$). This causes the signal paths to "bunch up" or compress in space, representing gravitational redshift.

### Rule 5: Frame Dragging (Lense-Thirring CA)
* **States ($N$):** $6$ states ($0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5$).
  * $0$: Empty space.
  * $1$: Left-moving matter ($M_L$).
  * $2$: Right-moving matter ($M_R$).
  * $3$: Static mass ($M_0$).
  * $4$: Clockwise-spinning mass (drag right, $J_R$).
  * $5$: Counterclockwise-spinning mass (drag left, $J_L$).
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  * If $S_t(x) \ge 3$ (massive bodies):
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = S_t(x)$$
  * Else (matter/vacuum cells):
    Compute the local Lense-Thirring drag flags:
    * $D_R(y) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(y-1) = 4 \lor S_t(y) = 4 \lor S_t(y+1) = 4)$
    * $D_L(y) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(y-1) = 5 \lor S_t(y) = 5 \lor S_t(y+1) = 5)$
    
    Define incoming signals:
    * $RecvR(x) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x-1) = 2 \land D_L(x-1) = 0)$
    * $RecvL(x) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x+1) = 1 \land D_R(x+1) = 0)$
    * $StayR(x) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x) = 2 \land D_L(x) > 0)$
    * $StayL(x) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x) = 1 \land D_R(x) > 0)$
    
    Next state:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} 2 & \text{if } RecvR(x) \lor StayR(x) \\ 1 & \text{if } RecvL(x) \lor StayL(x) \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** A rotating mass drags the local coordinate frame (frame dragging). If space is dragged to the right (clockwise mass), matter attempting to travel left is halted or reversed. This is modeled by blocking leftward steps and forcing particles to stand still when dragged in the opposite direction.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Light/matter particles propagate as straight diagonal lines, but when they pass next to a spinning mass (states 4 or 5), their propagation is delayed if they are counter-propagating, or accelerated if they are co-propagating.

### Rule 6: Schwarzschild Geodesics (Radial Fall)
* **States ($N$):** $7$ states ($0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6$).
  * $0$: Empty space.
  * $1$: Slow matter.
  * $2$: Left-moving fast matter.
  * $3$: Right-moving fast matter.
  * $4$: Event Horizon boundary.
  * $5$: Black Hole interior.
  * $6$: Singularity.
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  * If $S_t(x) \ge 4$ (black hole structure):
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = S_t(x)$$
  * Else:
    Let $InR(x) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x-1) = 3)$ and $InL(x) = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x+1) = 2)$.
    * If $S_t(x) = 0$:
      * If $InR(x) \land \neg InL(x)$:
        $$S_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } S_t(x+1) \ge 4 \\ 3 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
      * If $InL(x) \land \neg InR(x)$:
        $$S_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } S_t(x-1) \ge 4 \\ 2 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
      * If $InR(x) \land InL(x)$:
        $$S_{t+1}(x) = 1$$
      * Else:
        $$S_{t+1}(x) = 0$$
    * If $S_t(x) = 3$:
      $$S_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } S_t(x+1) \ge 4 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
    * If $S_t(x) = 2$:
      $$S_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } S_t(x-1) \ge 4 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
    * If $S_t(x) = 1$:
      $$S_{t+1}(x) = 0 \quad (\text{absorbed by the horizon})$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** For a distant observer, the coordinate velocity of a particle falling towards a Schwarzschild black hole event horizon approaches zero as $r \to r_s$. We model this radial deceleration by forcing fast matter particles ($2, 3$) to transition to slow matter ($1$) and stand still for one step before crossing the horizon boundary.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Infalling matter lines travel diagonally at constant velocity, but "bend" and pause right at the horizon boundary before disappearing, producing a distinct pile-up or accretion layer effect just outside the black hole.

### Rule 7: FLRW Expansion & Redshift (Cosmological CA)
* **States ($N$):** $8$ states ($0, 1, \dots, 7$).
  * The state $S_t(x)$ is split into:
    * Wave energy level: $W_t(x) = S_t(x) \pmod{4} \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$
    * Direction: $D_t(x) = \lfloor S_t(x) / 4 \rfloor \in \{0, 1\}$ ($0$ for Left-moving, $1$ for Right-moving)
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  * A right-mover is incoming if $S_t(x-1) \ge 4$.
    Let $W_{inR} = S_t(x-1) \pmod{4}$.
    The wave energy redshifts stochastically:
    $$W'_{inR} = \begin{cases} W_{inR} & \text{with probability } 0.85 \\ \max(0, W_{inR} - 1) & \text{with probability } 0.15 \end{cases}$$
  * A left-mover is incoming if $0 < S_t(x+1) < 4$.
    Let $W_{inL} = S_t(x+1)$.
    The wave energy redshifts stochastically:
    $$W'_{inL} = \begin{cases} W_{inL} & \text{with probability } 0.85 \\ \max(0, W_{inL} - 1) & \text{with probability } 0.15 \end{cases}$$
    
  The updated state is:
  $$S_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} W'_{inR} + 4 & \text{if } S_t(x-1) \ge 4 \\ W'_{inL} & \text{else if } 0 < S_t(x+1) < 4 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** The Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric describes an expanding universe. As waves propagate through expanding space, their wavelengths stretch, which is equivalent to energy loss (redshifting). Here, the energy level $W$ of a propagating wave packet decays stochastically as a function of the distance (time steps) travelled.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** High-energy wave packets start as bright diagonal trails. As they travel across the spacetime lattice, they stochastically step down to lower energy levels (changing colors) until they eventually fade completely into vacuum ($0$).

### Rule 8: Gravitational Collapse (Jeans Instability)
* **States ($N$):** $12$ states ($0, 1, \dots, 11$).
  * $0$: Vacuum.
  * $1 \dots 10$: Increasing density.
  * $11$: Core state (black hole/star).
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=2$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-2, x-1, x, x+1, x+2\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  Let $M(y) = 11$ if $S_t(y) = 11$ else $S_t(y)$. For each cell $y$, compute net gravitational pull:
  $$F_{net}(y) = \left(M(y+1) + \frac{1}{4} M(y+2)\right) - \left(M(y-1) + \frac{1}{4} M(y-2)\right)$$
  
  Movement flags:
  * $OutR(y) = \mathbb{I}(F_{net}(y) > 2 \land S_t(y) > 0 \land S_t(y) < 11)$
  * $OutL(y) = \mathbb{I}(F_{net}(y) < -2 \land S_t(y) > 0 \land S_t(y) < 11)$
  
  If $S_t(x) = 11$:
  $$S_{t+1}(x) = 11$$
  Else:
  $$S_{new} = S_t(x) - OutR(x) - OutL(x) + OutR(x-1) + OutL(x+1)$$
  $$S_{t+1}(x) = \min(11, \max(0, S_{new}))$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** In Newtonian cosmology, the Jeans instability describes how gravitational attraction overcomes thermal pressure, causing matter to clump together. The attraction is modeled by calculating a discretized spatial derivative of the matter distribution with $1/d^2$ scaling, causing density to flow towards local gravitational maxima.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Starting from a random distribution with slight noise, matter collapses into a few highly dense cores (state 11), leaving empty voids (state 0) between them, creating a cellular representation of the cosmic web.

### Rule 9: Causal Light Cones (Penrose Horizon CA)
* **States ($N$):** $8$ states ($0, 1, \dots, 7$).
  * The state $S_t(x)$ is split into:
    * Signal state: $Sig_t(x) = S_t(x) \pmod{4} \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$
    * Spacetime geometry tilt: $Tilt(x) = \lfloor S_t(x) / 4 \rfloor \in \{0, 1\}$
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  The geometry is static:
  $$Tilt_{t+1}(x) = Tilt(x)$$
  
  Define incoming signals from neighbors:
  * $Sig_{inR} = Sig_t(x-1)$ if $Tilt(x-1) = 0$ else $0$
  * $Sig_{inL} = Sig_t(x+1)$
  
  The updated signal is:
  $$Sig_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} Sig_{inL} & \text{if } Tilt(x) = 1 \\ \max(Sig_{inR}, Sig_{inL}) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
  
  Recombine:
  $$S_{t+1}(x) = Sig_{t+1}(x) + 4 \cdot Tilt_{t+1}(x)$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** In Kruskal-Szekeres coordinates, or general Penrose diagrams of a black hole, the light cones tilt as they approach the singularity, meaning that all causal paths are directed inward. In this CA, when $Tilt=1$, the local light cone is restricted: it cannot receive right-propagating signals, forcing all signals to travel exclusively leftward.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** In flat regions ($Tilt=0$), signals spread symmetrically, forming classic triangular light cones. Upon crossing into the tilted region ($Tilt=1$), the light cone shears, forcing the wave to propagate in one direction only.

### Rule 10: Einstein-Rosen Bridge (Wormhole CA)
* **States ($N$):** $10$ states ($0, 1, \dots, 9$).
  * $0$: Empty space.
  * $1$: Left-moving particle.
  * $2$: Right-moving particle.
  * $3$: Particle collision (stationary).
  * $4$: Wormhole Mouth A ($W_A$).
  * $5$: Wormhole Mouth B ($W_B$).
  * $6 \dots 9$: Static boundary states.
* **Neighborhood ($N(x)$):** Radius $r=1$ including the cell itself:
  $$N(x) = \{x-1, x, x+1\}$$
* **Transition Function:**
  Let $x_A$ and $x_B$ be the constant positions where $S_0(x_A) = 4$ and $S_0(x_B) = 5$.
  * If $S_t(x) \ge 4$:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = S_t(x)$$
  * Else:
    * For $x = x_A - 1$:
      $$InR = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x-1) = 2), \quad InL = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x_B + 1) = 1)$$
    * For $x = x_A + 1$:
      $$InR = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x_B - 1) = 2), \quad InL = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x+1) = 1)$$
    * For $x = x_B - 1$:
      $$InR = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x-1) = 2), \quad InL = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x_A + 1) = 1)$$
    * For $x = x_B + 1$:
      $$InR = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x_A - 1) = 2), \quad InL = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x+1) = 1)$$
    * For any other cell $x$:
      $$InR = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x-1) = 2), \quad InL = \mathbb{I}(S_t(x+1) = 1)$$
      
    Update cell $x$:
    $$S_{t+1}(x) = \begin{cases} 2 & \text{if } InR = 1 \land InL = 0 \\ 1 & \text{if } InL = 1 \land InR = 0 \\ 3 & \text{if } InR = 1 \land InL = 1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
* **Mathematical Rationale:** An Einstein-Rosen bridge is a topological shortcut between two regions of spacetime. By defining non-local transition rules at the boundaries of the wormhole mouths ($x_A$ and $x_B$), we mathematically stitch the lattice coordinates together, making them causally adjacent.
* **Expected Visual Behavior:** Particles travel linearly across the grid. When they hit Mouth A, they disappear and reappear at Mouth B, continuing their original direction, creating a direct visual demonstration of topological wormholes.
